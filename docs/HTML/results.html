Implications

Is it the same everywhere?

We analysed the same trends in many other states to verify that our hypothesis (i.e., that education plays a positive role in defining one’s income).

Our research has led to the confirmation of a positive relationship between these two variables, and this is shown below.

Examples of states with the GDP per capita plotted against education attainment at a state level are plotted below, with individual counties as our data points

Figure 1: scatterplots for the 3 highest populated states in the US (California, Texas and Florida), with their correlation coefficients of 0.23, 0.10, 0.09 respectively.

By finding the correlation coefficient for each state, through compiling all the counties within each state, we test our hypothesis.

The first impression we get from these first three scatterplots, is that the percent of the population with a Bachelor’s degree is observably unchanging, compared to GDP per Capita.

Despite this, we found that average correlation coefficient to be approximately +0.118. This is not evidence of a strong relationship between our two variables, but it does work in favour of our theory. We now move on to examine the 5 states with the highest correlation coefficient to further dissect the data.

We believe that our calculation of the average correlation coefficient between GDP per Capita and Education attainment, of 0.118, does a positive relationship. However, 0.118 is still very close to zero, and does not indicate that there is a strong relationship. As for the nature of this relationship, further analysis is required to determine whether GDP per Capita is a determinant of the Higher education attainment of states, or the other way around.

Is it the same for everyone?

Although so far we have seen a positive trend in many states and slightly at the national level between average higher educational attainment and national income among counties, it does not show the whole story. Is this trend potentially stronger when looking at counties with a population that is mostly white? Or one that is mostly black?

While we did not have enough time to do further statistical testing to compare how strongly there is a relationship is between race, education and income, we are already able to see some preliminary trends. Our analysis have allowed us to suggest that this is a potentially important area to further research since the gains to education for a county might be different depending on the characteristics of its population. This would imply that there is a variable other than education leading both national income and education to increase in those counties, making education not as important as this third factor.

Here we look at counties in North Carolina, and see that the overall trend when looking at all races between education and national income differs when we only select counties with majority white, black or native populations.

Research Limitations

  1. The first clear limitation is the spatial one: we only focused on the US as one of the largest and easiest countries to analyze due to its economy. This trend, however, may not be visible in other countries. → a further point should be the one of expanding the sources.
  2. The second limitation concerns the causal inference. What comes first? Education or salary: does richer families allow their children to study more or is a higher level of education that allowed individuals to earn more and jump from one social class to another. → having data on individuals’ level of attainment and their salary would have helped instead of having access to aggregate data only.
  3. Lastly, we could expand our research by further analyzing whether adverse events (economic crisis, pandemic, recession, wars) influence the College percentage. For that, we would need more evidence and data on how much different events affect the economy and education.
